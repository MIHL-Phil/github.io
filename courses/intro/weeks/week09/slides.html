What is reality?

Physical Realism: Physical things are the only things that’s real

  • Physical things are real.
  • Only physical things are real.
  • Virtual things are not real.

Virtual Realism:

  • Physical things are physically real
  • Virtual things are virtually real

As I understand it, virtual realism is the thesis that virtual reality is genuine reality, with emphasis especially on the view that virtual objects are real and not an illusion.

Simulation Realism: If we are in a simulation, then the things around us are real

Virtual digitialism: Objects in virtual reality are digital (not physical) objects

Reality and Realities

Defining Reality: 3 Points

  • Everything that exists, the entire cosmos
  • A world or worlds, either multiverse, virtual spaces, physical or virtual
  • A property, rigidity, property of being real

The no-illusion view in the history of philosophy

What about objects in a Cartesian scenario? What are their natures?

  • Descartes’s evil-demon scenario?
  • Hilary Putnam’s brain-in-a-vat scenario?

How does David Chalmers respond to the skeptic in these scenarios?

Descartes’s evil-demon and Hilary Putnam’s brain-in-a-vat scenarios are sceptical views about our ability to know facts about our world. The sceptic argues that we do not know anything. Descartes famously offers a solution suggesting that first, he is a thinking thing, and then uses reason to deduce that God would not want him to be deceived. Still, according to the epistemic sceptic, it is possible that we do not know anything and each offers varying rebuttals. The following thought experiments show us some possible complications and responses.

In Descartes’s evil-demon thought experiment, Descartes argues that it is possible that an evil demon has deceived us. In Hillary Putnam’s brain-in-a-vat scenario, he imagines someone waking up in a lab. Unfortunately for the hapless individual however, this person does not realize that he has awoken in a lab. This is because this individual has been connected to a machine that feeds him the content of all his perceptions. According to Descartes, all of our perceptions are based on an illusion. Similarly in Putnam’s example, the perceptions of the hapless individual are being fed to them by a machine. Therefore, the contents of all perceptions are all being manufactured by the demon or created by a machine. It is possible that we are the hapless individuals and would not know otherwise. Any belief that we have, that is based on the illusion created by the evil demon will be false according to the sceptic. Using this possibility, Descartes, like Putnam, argues that all of our beliefs about the outside world are false. However, Chalmers defends what he calls the no-illusion view. According to Chalmer’s view, if we are living in a world manufactured by the evil demon, then it is not true that the objects of our perceptions are illusions, but rather that the objects of our perceptions are real. Their underlying nature however may not be what we thought it was.

If one is in a perfect simulation, then the things inside that simulation are perfectly real according to Chalmers. This is because according to the no-illusion view, in a skeptical scenario such as the evil-demon or even the brain-in-a-vat scenario, an object’s nature is largely as it appears, the flower that I am perceiving, even though created by the machine, really is a flower.

Descartes’s response is often called a form of foundationalism grounded in what is called rationalism. Knowledge begins with some base or foundation. For Descartes, this base or foundation is the cogito “I think therefore I am”. George Berkeley offers a response that is similar to Chalmers’s own view. According to Berkeley, appearance is reality. Therefore if I perceive a given object, then that object is reality for me. Whether I am living in a dream-like state, an illusion created by an evil demon, or a simulation created by a scientist.

Dave Chalmers: A virtual reality environment is an immersive, interactive, and computer-generated space.

  • Interactive: means that there’s two-way interaction between users and the environment, and among objects in the environment.

  • Computer-Generated: a computer is generating the signals that are sent to our sensory systems.

  • Immersive: means that we experience the environment as a world all around us, with ourselves present at the center.

    • psychologically immersive: occupying all of our attention in a sort of flow state
    • perceptually immersive: the world as a three-dimensional world surrounding us.
    • audiovisual immersion: the environments look and sound as if you’re immersed in them.
    • bodily immersion: the environments look and sound as if you’re immersed in them.
    • full immersion: “A fully immersive or full-dive VR is one that users apprehend with all their senses, as if they’re physically inhabiting the environment, and where no trace of the ordinary physical environment remains.”

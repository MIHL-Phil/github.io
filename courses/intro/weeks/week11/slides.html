We are already familiar with dualism. Logical behaviorism, posited by Gilbert Ryle (1949) is that it is a mistake to think of the “mental” as a set of “inner causes of behavior”, but rather as a set of dispositions to behave and patters of behavior. We might also think that mental states are identical to states or events of the brain. Functionalists classify a thing by what it does. Mental states then are just states with a particular role, whatever they are and theory-theorists argue that mental state concepts come from their position in the causal structure and functioning of the mind.

  • Weak Dualism: Mental properties are not physical

Logical Behaviorism \(\Rightarrow\) Physicalism

Forms of physicalism

  • Supervenience
  • Emergence
  • Intelligibility

What the above says is that if logical behaviorism is true, then physicalism is true.

Recall that according to @bott03, logical behaviorism is the view that an observation of our internal experiences:

  • feelings, emotions, beliefs, and thoughts,
  • should not lead to a discussion about whether these experiences, the causes of the mental states, are grounded in physical reality or not.
  • Rather it is a talk about a disposition to behavior.

However, logical behaviorism was only offered as an account of conceptual analysis.

What logical behaviorism says is that “mental events or states should actually be construed as ‘iffy’ or conditional statements about people’s actual and possible behavior”.

Logical behaviorism is an important part of understanding our experiences and more importantly, the divergent nature of our subjective and objective experiences. It gives us a way to account for the difference between our experiences and observations where we experience the world subjectively, but our observations do not seem to support those experiences.

A challenge in building a physicalist theory, is developing theories corresponding with the physical.

  • What does it mean to be physical?
    • Chalmers gives an account of the physical that includes small constituent components of matter and accompanying facts about how those constituent components of matter are arranged.
    • What ever theory we come up with, needs to explain the difference between our experiences and our observations however.

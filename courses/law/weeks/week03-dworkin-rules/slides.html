Jurisprudence: Positivism Debates

Hart’s Union of Rules, Dworkin’s Model, Exclusive & Inclusive Positivism

What are legal rights and obligations?

Legal obligation is determined by applying “the law” to the facts of the case

  • But what is the law?
  • Why are we concerned with what the law “says”?
  • Does the law determine our obligations or merely point to our obligations?
  • If it only points to our obligations, than what are our obligations rooted in?
  • What then, are our legal obligations ultimately grounded in?

Recap of Previous Topics

  • Kretzmann’s Lex Iniusta Non Est Lex: Unjust laws not true laws.
  • Fuller’s Speluncean Explorers: Judicial philosophies in action.
  • Bentham’s Principles of Morals: Utilitarian positivism; law from utility, separate from morality.

High-level continuity: Ties to positivism’s evolution and critiques.

Introduction to HLA Hart

  • Key Work: The Concept of Law (1961).
  • Refines Bentham/Austin: Beyond sovereign commands.
  • Law as social artifact; rule of recognition central.

Foundation for positivist debates.

Hart’s Primary Rules

  • Impose duties (e.g., “Do not steal”).
  • Backed by sanctions; oblige behavior.
  • Limitations: Uncertainty, static, inefficient in primitives.

Basic content; links to Bentham’s utility.

Hart’s Secondary Rules

  • Meta-rules: Address primary deficiencies.
  • Recognition: Validates law (e.g., constitution).
  • Change: Modify/repeal (e.g., legislation).
  • Adjudication: Resolve disputes (e.g., courts).

Framework for mature systems.

Union of Primary and Secondary Rules

  • Law = Content (primary) + Structure (secondary).
  • Separates validity from morality (contra Kretzmann).
  • Addresses Speluncean: Judges apply rules interpretively.

Echoes Bentham’s separation thesis.

Introduction to Ronald Dworkin

  • Key Work: The Model of Rules (1967).
  • Critiques positivism: Incomplete for hard cases.
  • Law beyond rules; integrates principles.

Challenges Hart; closer to natural law elements.

Dworkin’s Rules vs. Principles

  • Rules: All-or-nothing; strict application.
  • Principles: Weighted; balance in adjudication (e.g., fairness).
  • Critique: Positivism ignores principles’ role.

Links to Speluncean: Judges weigh principles.

Dworkin’s Model of Rules

  • Flaw: Law not just explicit rules; includes principles/policies.
  • Hercules Judge: Interprets coherently, fitting principles.
  • Blurs law/morality (contra Bentham/Hart).

Interpretive; morality informs validity in hard cases.

Comparison: Hart vs. Dworkin

Aspect Hart (Positivism) Dworkin (Interpretivism)
Law’s Nature Rules (primary + secondary) Rules + principles
Validity Rule of recognition (pedigree) Best moral fit
Hard Cases Discretion (open texture) Principled interpretation
Morality Separated Integrated

Builds on Bentham/Fuller.

Exclusive Legal Positivism (Marmor)

  • Core: Validity exhausted by sources; no moral content.
  • Arguments:
    • Conventional foundation: Constitutive, not epistemic.
    • Authority (Raz): Directives identifiable without dependent reasons.
  • Denies inclusive: No middle ground with Dworkin.

Defends strong sources thesis.

Inclusive Legal Positivism (Himma)

  • Core: Possible moral criteria of validity (incorporation).
  • Theses:
    • Social Fact: Law from social artifacts.
    • Conventionality: Weak (convergence); Strong (duty-imposing).
    • Separability: No necessary moral overlap.
  • Allows necessity/sufficiency components.

Soft positivism; accommodates moral constraints.

Conceptual Foundations: Social Fact Thesis

  • Law as social creation/artifact.
  • Austin: Sovereign commands + sanctions.
  • Hart: Rule of recognition + acceptance/obedience.
  • Explains validity/authority via social facts.

Shared positivist base.

Conventionality Theses

  • Weak: Validity criteria from official convention (SCA/coordination/constitutive).
  • Strong: Rule of recognition imposes duties.
  • Debates: Raz/Marmor reject strong; Coleman/Hart affirm.

Explains normativity.

Separability Thesis

  • Law/morality conceptually distinct.
  • No necessary moral validity criteria.
  • Allows contingent overlap (inclusive view).

Foundation; denies natural law necessity.

Incorporation Thesis Overview

  • Moral principles can be validity criteria.
  • Necessity: Consistency with morals required.
  • Sufficiency: Moral content validates.
  • Historical: Hart vs. Fuller/Dworkin.

Inclusive’s key claim.

Exclusive vs. Inclusive: Key Debates

Aspect Exclusive (Raz/Marmor/Shapiro) Inclusive (Hart/Coleman/Waluchow)
Validity Sources only Sources + morality possible
Morality External; no incorporation Can constrain/validate
Authority Pre-emptive; identifiable Compatible; non-pre-emptive OK
Function Guides via sources Guides; allows moral guidance

Core divide in positivism.

Connections Across Topics

  • Bentham: Positivism’s utilitarian roots; separation.
  • Hart: Refines; union of rules; allows incorporation.
  • Dworkin: Critiques rules-only; principles integrate morality.
  • Exclusive/Inclusive: Debate incorporation’s coherence.

Evolution from positivism to critiques.

Discussion Questions (30-Minute Focus)

  • Does incorporation undermine law’s authority? (Raz vs. Coleman)
  • Can morality guide without pre-emption? (Waluchow/Hurd)
  • Exclusive or inclusive: Which better fits constitutional practice?
  • Implications for hard cases like Speluncean/Riggs?

Allocate ~10 min per question; encourage debate.

Dworkin, and a Summary of Initial Issues

They think that when we speak of “the law,” we mean a set of timeless rules stocked in some conceptual warehouse awaiting discovery by judges, and that when we speak of legal obligation we mean the invisible chains these mysterious rules somehow drape around us.

  • Rules
  • Legal Obligations

Tenets of Positivism

  1. The law of a community is a set of special rules used by the community directly or indirectly for the purpose of determining which behavior will be punished or coerced by the public power.
  2. The set of these valid legal rules is exhaustive of “the law,” so that if someone’s case is not clearly covered by such a rule (because there is none that seems appropriate, or those that seem appropriate are vague, or for some other reason) then that case cannot be decided by “applying the law.”
  3. To say that someone has a “legal obligation” is to say that his case falls under a valid legal rule that requires him to do or to for- bear from doing something.

Austin and Obligation

obligation as lying under a rule, a rule as a general command, and a command as an expression of desire that others behave in a particular way, backed by the power and will to enforce that expression in the event of disobedience.

  1. Lies under a rule
  2. Rule must be a general command
  3. Command is an expression of desire about other’s behavior
  4. Backed by power and enforcement

Austin and first tenet

  1. The law of a community is a set of special rules used by the community directly or indirectly for the purpose of determining which behavior will be punished or coerced by the public power.

It asserts the first tenet of positivism, that the law is a set of rules specially selected to govern public order, and offers a simple factual test-what has the sovereign commanded?-as the sole criterion for identifying those special rules.

Some problems

  1. Who is the Sovereign?
  • Who is the sovereign in a modern democracy?
  • If the people, then how do we determine the will of the people?
  • Who are the people?
  1. Obligation vs. Being Obliged

Hart

  • Distinguishes between two kinds of rules
  • Rules are normative: sets a standard of behavior
  • Authority to issue rules only come from other rules

Two sources of a rule’s authority (Rule of Recognition)

  1. A rule may become binding upon a group of people because that group through its practices accepts the rule as a standard for its conduct. (because it is accepted)
  2. A rule may also become binding in quite a different way, namely by being enacted in conformity with some secondary rule that stipulates that rules so enacted shall be binding. (because it is valid)

However, binding force of rule of recognition depends on its acceptance and is not in itself valid.

Hart finds their authority in the background of constitutional standards against which they act, constitutional standards that have been accepted, in the form of a fundamental rule of recognition, by the community which they govern.

  • Provides no test
  • Admits of fuzzy areas around the law

Dworkin’s Response

My strategy will be organized around the fact that when lawyers reason or dispute about legal rights and obligations, particularly in those hard cases when our problems with these concepts seem most acute, they make use of standards that do not function as rules, but operate differently as principles, policies, and other sorts of standards. Positivism, I shall argue, is a model of and for a system of rules, and its central notion of a single fundamental test for law forces us to miss the important roles of these standards that are not rules.

Atoms of decision

  • Principles (standards)
  • Rules

Riggs v. Palmer

In 1889 a New York court, in the famous case of Riggs v. Palmer, 9 had to decide whether an heir named in the will of his grandfather could inherit under that will, even though he had murdered his grandfather to do so.
